SpaceShip

A spacecraft is a vehicle that flies in space. It can carry astronauts, cargo, or instruments to their destination, or it can be the destination. The International Space Station is a spacecraft, just like the smaller vehicles that deliver crew and cargo to it.

SOLAR SYSTEM

Solar System

The solar system is the vast region of space dominated by the Sun and everything bound to it by gravity. It includes eight planets—Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune—along with their moons, asteroids, comets, and dwarf planets like Pluto. Formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a rotating cloud of gas and dust, the solar system is organized with rocky planets closer to the Sun and gas and ice giants farther away. At its center, the Sun provides the light and energy that sustain life on Earth. Beyond Neptune lies the Kuiper Belt and the distant Oort Cloud, where countless icy bodies orbit. The solar system is our cosmic neighborhood, a small but wondrous part of the vast universe.

PLANET OF OUR SOLAR SYSTEM

Mercury

Mercury is the smallest, innermost planet and almost airless, covered in impact craters, and resembles our Moon’s surface. Its large iron-rich core makes it the densest small planet. Surface temperatures swing from –180 °C at night to +430 °C in sunlight. Its year lasts just 88 Earth days, and it has no moons.

Distance: - About 57.9 million km from the Sun.

Venus

Venus is nearly the same size as Earth but extremely hot due to its thick CO₂ atmosphere, causing a runaway greenhouse effect. The surface temperature can reach 465 °C, and it has crushing atmospheric pressure 90 times greater than Earth’s. Winds in the upper atmosphere exceed 220 mph, and the day is longer than its year. Its surface is hidden by clouds of carbon dioxide and sulfuric acid.

Earth

Earth is the only known planet with life, a balanced climate, and liquid water covering 71% of its surface. Its atmosphere is mainly nitrogen and oxygen, protecting living beings from harmful rays. Earth’s structure includes a core, mantle, and thin crust supporting oceans and continents. The axis tilt creates seasons as it orbits the Sun in 365 days.

Distance: - About 149.6 million km from the Sun.

Mars

The Red Planet has rusty iron-rich soil, thin air (95% CO₂), and massive volcanoes like Olympus Mons. It has two moons, Phobos and Deimos, and experiences dust storms and seasonal changes. Mars has polar ice caps and evidence of ancient water. Its surface is mainly highlands in the south and plains in the north.

Distance: - About 227.9 million km from the Sun.

ABOUT SUN & MOON

Sun

A supernova is a powerful and spectacular explosion that marks the end of a massive star’s life cycle. When a star exhausts its nuclear fuel, its core collapses under gravity, releasing an enormous burst of energy that outshines entire galaxies for a short time. These explosions scatter heavy elements like iron, gold, and oxygen into space, enriching the universe and contributing to the formation of new stars and planets.

Moon

The Moon is Earth’s only natural moon. It orbits our planet at an average distance of about 384,400 kilometers, influencing tides and stabilizing Earth’s rotation. Formed billions of years ago, likely from debris after a massive collision, the Moon’s surface is covered with craters, mountains, and plains of solidified lava called maria. It has no atmosphere, weather, or liquid water, but its surface holds valuable clues about the early solar system.

Space Universer

Black hole

A black hole forms when a massive star collapses under gravity. Its pull is so strong that not even light can escape. Around it lies the event horizon, a point of no return. Invisible yet powerful, it shapes galaxies and space-time itself.

Nebula

A nebula is a vast cloud of gas and dust found in outer space. It can birth new stars or form from dying ones. Some glow, some reflect light, and others block starlight. They recycle matter, creating beauty and life across the cosmos.

SuperNova

A supernova is a massive star’s powerful and brilliant explosion. It releases immense energy, outshining entire galaxies for a moment. These blasts create heavy elements that form new stars and planets. They end a star’s life but spark new beginnings in the cosmos.

Galaxy

A galaxy is a huge collection of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter. They come in spiral, elliptical, or irregular shapes. The Milky Way, our home, holds billions of stars and planets. Galaxies cluster together, forming the structure of the universe.

Andromeda Galaxy

The Andromeda Galaxy is the closest large galaxy to the Milky Way. It lies about 2.5 million light-years from Earth in the night sky. Containing over a trillion stars, it’s much larger than our galaxy. In billions of years, Andromeda and the Milky Way will merge into one.

observable Universe

The observable universe spans about 93 billion light-years across. It includes everything whose light has reached us since the Big Bang. Countless galaxies, stars, and planets exist within this vast space. Beyond it, the universe may continue infinitely, unseen and unknown.

INTERSTELLAR COMET

1I/ʻOumuamua (2017)

ʻOumuamua was the first interstellar object ever discovered passing through our solar system. It was detected in October 2017 by the Pan-STARRS telescope in Hawaii. It came from outside our solar system and was moving very fast on a straight, hyperbolic path. ʻOumuamua showed no clear comet tail, so scientists think it was more like a rocky or metallic object. It left the solar system and will never return.

2I/Borisov (2019)

2I/Borisov was the second interstellar object and the first confirmed interstellar comet. It was discovered in August 2019 by amateur astronomer Gennady Borisov. Unlike ʻOumuamua, it had a clear comet tail, gas, and dust. This proved it was a true comet from another star system. After passing the Sun, it also left the solar system forever.

3I/Atlas (2025)

The object was first detected on July 1, 2025 by the ATLAS survey telescope in Chile as it moved toward the inner solar system. Before being formally recognized, earlier images from other telescopes showed it even farther out, helping confirm its fast, hyperbolic path. Because of this path and its high speed, astronomers knew it had to be coming from interstellar space — not from within our own solar system.

KEY ASTRONOMICAL STRUCTURES

Solar System

The Solar System is the Sun and all celestial bodies, including planets and asteroids, bound to it by gravity. It formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a rotating cloud of gas and dust. It contains eight major planets and is located in the Milky Way Galaxy.

Supernova

A Supernova is a massive, violent explosion marking the dramatic end of a star's life. It happens when a massive star collapses or a white dwarf reaches a critical mass. These events briefly outshine entire galaxies and scatter heavy elements across space.

Nebula

A Nebula is a vast cloud of interstellar gas (hydrogen and helium) and dust. These clouds are known as "star nurseries" where new stars and planetary systems are formed. They are categorized as emission, reflection, or dark, depending on how they interact with light.

Galaxy

A Galaxy is a colossal, gravitationally bound system of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter. Galaxies are classified by shape (spiral, elliptical, irregular) and range from millions to trillions of stars. Our own galaxy is the Milky Way, a barred spiral.

THE BIG BANG
Beginnings Of Everything

The Big Bang is the scientific theory that explains how the universe began. About 13.8 billion years ago, the universe started from a very hot and extremely dense point. Suddenly, space began to expand, and this expansion is called the Big Bang. As the universe expanded and cooled, matter and energy formed. Over time, stars, galaxies, and planets came into existence. Even today, the universe is still expanding.

THE HIERARCHICAL STRUCTURE
OF THE UNIVERSE

Our Solar System

The Sun and all the bodies, including planets and moons, gravitationally bound in orbit around it.

Milky Way Galaxy

Our host, a barred spiral galaxy containing billions of stars, gas, dust, and a central supermassive black hole.

Local Galactic Group

A small, gravitationally bound cluster of $\approx 50$ galaxies, centered around the Milky Way and Andromeda.

Virgo Supercluster

A massive collection of thousands of galaxies that contains our Local Group and is centered on the dense Virgo Cluster.

Laniakea Supercluster

A vast, dynamically defined structure of $\approx 100,000$ galaxies whose movements flow toward the Great Attractor.

Observable Universe

The entire spherical region of space from which light has had time to reach Earth since the Big Bang.

TIMELINE

(13.8 Billion Years Ago)
Big Bang

The Big Bang is the beginning of the universe. About 13.8 billion years ago, everything was very hot and dense. Suddenly, space started expanding in all directions. This expansion created time, space, energy, and matter.

(First Few Seconds)
Early Universe

Just after the Big Bang, the universe was extremely hot. There were no stars, planets, or galaxies at that time. The universe was filled only with energy and tiny particles. Slowly, it started cooling down.

(First 3 Minutes)
Formation of Particles

As the universe cooled, energy changed into matter. Small particles like protons, neutrons, and electrons were formed. These particles are the basic building blocks of matter. They later helped in forming atoms.

(380,000 Years After Big Bang)
Formation of Atoms

After some time, particles joined together to form atoms. Hydrogen and helium were the first atoms created. Light could now travel freely through space. The universe became clearer and darker often called the "Dark Ages" before stars lit up.

(~400 Million Years After Big Bang)
First Stars

Gravity pulled large clouds of gas together. These clouds became very hot and formed the first stars. Stars produced light and heat. Inside stars, heavier elements were created.

(~1 Billion Years After Big Bang)
Formation of Galaxies

Stars grouped together to form galaxies. A galaxy contains billions of stars. Our home galaxy is called the Milky Way. Galaxies spread throughout the universe.

(4.6 Billion Years Ago)
Solar System Formation

About 4.6 billion years ago, the Sun was formed. Planets formed around the Sun from dust and gas. Earth is one of these planets. This system is called the solar system.

(~3.8 Billion Years Ago)
Earth and Life

Earth slowly cooled down after its formation. Water appeared on the surface of Earth. Simple life forms developed in water. Over time, life became more complex.

(Now)
Present Universe

Today, the universe has billions of galaxies and stars. Planets, moons, and other space objects exist everywhere. The universe is still expanding. Scientists continue to study space.

(Billions of Years from Now)
Future of the Universe

In the future, stars will slowly lose their energy. Galaxies may move farther apart due to the expansion of the universe. The universe will become colder and darker. Fewer new stars will be formed.

(Trillions of Years from Now)
End of the Universe

One possible end is called the Big Freeze or Heat Death. In this case, all stars burn out completely. The universe becomes dark, cold, and silent. Everything slowly fades away.
